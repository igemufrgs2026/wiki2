PREPARATION

October 2023 – When the dream began

We met with the previous UFRGS iGEM team (GlyFloat), seeking guidance on how to build a team within our institutional and regional reality: where to start, how to organize ourselves, and which paths were realistically possible. From these conversations came practical advice, institutional memory, and valuable contacts.

We also met with Giulio Braatz, a former iGEM Ambassador in Brazil, who helped us understand the overall structure, expectations, and spirit of the competition, allowing us to finally see the path ahead more clearly.

Shortly after, a meeting with Cristiane Toledo, one of the iGEM Design League organizers, introduced us to the Design League as a feasible and strategic first step into iGEM. One message became clear: to move forward, we needed to find a local problem that could be meaningfully addressed using synthetic biology.

Then-current goal: to take part in the iGEM Design League.

FINDING A PROJECT

November 2023 – From ideas to purpose

We held multiple brainstorming sessions, exploring a wide range of ideas, from fungal pigments to pest control in olive crops. Each idea reflected curiosity, but none yet felt anchored in an urgent local need.

That changed during an informal conversation with a former iGEMer from UFRGS, Ângelo Angonezi, who asked a simple but transformative question: “Have you heard about 2,4-D contamination in grape crops?”

At that moment, the problem revealed itself. The pieces began to align.

A real problem had been found.

DEVELOPING THE PROJECT

December 2023 – The first steps

We began internal discussions on how synthetic biology could help farmers facing 2,4-D contamination. The challenge was complex, but the motivation was strong: translating molecular tools into real-world impact.

January 2024 – Building foundations

We focused on organizing the group and strengthening communication. At the same time, we launched our science communication efforts on social media, creating the Instagram account @igem.ufrgs as our first bridge to the public.

February 2024 – Understanding the problem deeply

Team members dedicated themselves to understanding how 2,4-D affects the environment, society, and the local economy. This was the month when theory met reality.

We met with Alexandre Angonezi, executive director of Garibaldi, one of the largest wineries in southern Brazil, who provided a technical and economic perspective on the issue. This conversation confirmed the relevance and urgency of the problem.

We identified the Campanha region of Rio Grande do Sul as a hotspot for 2,4-D contamination, due to its proximity to soybean crops where the herbicide is heavily used.

A meeting with local winemakers Mara and Luís, from Valle da Niagara (Espumoso, RS), brought the producers’ perspective. They spoke openly about their fear of 2,4-D drift. Although they had not yet been affected, they described preventive barrier strategies, such as vegetation buffers and physical covers, and the constant tension between soybean and grape growers.

As Luís stated: “2,4-D is the great villain for grapevines.”

Just as the project was gaining momentum, we faced a major setback.

The iGEM Design League was cancelled.

REROUTING

March 2024 – Finding new motivation

Rather than abandoning the project, we chose to persist. The team decided to aim for participation in the iGEM Grand Jamboree in 2025, using 2024 as a year for structured project development.

Our initial biological goal was to design a 2,4-D degradation system based on known catabolic pathways used by microorganisms — an ambitious idea that would later evolve.

April 2024 – Defining an identity

We presented the project to incoming biotechnology students, opening the door to new members and fresh perspectives.

During this phase, we identified a metabolic pathway for 2,4-D degradation in Cupriavidus pinatubonensis. At the same time, we reaffirmed our commitment to the 2,4-D issue and defined the visual identity of the project, including our logo.

May 2024 – When nature reminded us of our limits

Severe floods struck Rio Grande do Sul, paralyzing university activities for nearly two months. Several team members were directly affected, and all project activities were suspended.

June 2024 – Just keep going

As activities slowly resumed, the team restarted meetings and shifted focus toward building connections with specialists who could help refine the project.

July–August 2024 – Refining the project through expert insight

Some team members attended the 1st Brazilian Congress on Synthetic Biology, expanding our technical and conceptual horizons.

We met with Prof. Aldo Merotto Junior (UFRGS), a specialist in herbology, who explained the mechanisms of 2,4-D absorption in plants. During this discussion, a crucial insight emerged:

“A practical and fast biosensor would already be a major advance in controlling 2,4-D drift.”

This perspective opened new possibilities: could monitoring and detection be a more effective first line of action than degradation?

Subsequent meetings with Prof. Catarine Markus (UFRGS) and Prof. Rafael Roehrs (UNIPAMPA) challenged the feasibility, stability, cost, and implementation of a degradation system and further strengthened the case for a biosensor-based approach.

BIOLOGY TAKES THE LEAD

September–October 2024 – A clear definition

The project reached a turning point: we would build a biosensor.

We defined a detection system based on the cadR/cadA regulatory elements and began vector construction. With the biological design in place, we also initiated fundraising efforts, as institutional funding was not available.

November 2024 – Expanding Human Practices

A meeting with Helio Marchioro, president of FECOVINHO, introduced the political and regulatory dimensions of 2,4-D use. He explained existing legislation and its failures, highlighting the ongoing political struggle for effective regulation.

In the same period, we spoke with Mateus Wanderer, an agronomist working at the municipality of Jaguari — a city located in the Campanha region and one of the sites where discussions on 2,4-D were taking place. He highlighted a critical practical limitation: conventional 2,4-D analyses, such as HPLC, are extremely costly in terms of time, infrastructure, and financial resources for municipalities and producers. According to him, a practical, fast, and low-cost biosensor would be an extremely valuable tool for monitoring and decision-making in real-world agricultural settings.

Through these connections, we expanded our network of winemakers, technicians, researchers, and policymakers. We were invited to our first formal, multi-sector meeting to discuss 2,4-D contamination.

December 2024 – Voices from the field

Two team members attended the workshop “Hormonal Herbicides: Impacts of Drift on Agricultural Production.” Alongside other participants, we signed a formal letter to the state government addressing the 2,4-D issue.

Government representatives acknowledged a lack of resources for monitoring and enforcement. Grape growers shared testimonies marked by fear and uncertainty — not only for their crops, but for their families and livelihoods. It became clear that this was not merely an economic issue, but one of survival.

2025 – THE YEAR (OR NOT)

January–March 2025 – Sustaining the project

Fundraising became a central concern. Two major initiatives were launched: the online course “Synthetic Biology for Beginners” and the in-person 1st Gaúcho Symposium on Biomolecular Engineering, both developed throughout the year.

We gave interviews to a local TV station, bringing public attention to the 2,4-D problem and presenting our proposed solution.

A meeting with the university’s president confirmed the lack of institutional funding, but also secured political and academic visibility for the project.

April 2025 – Big goals, difficult decisions

Given our financial reality, we decided to officially compete in iGEM only in 2026. With the biological design established, we redirected efforts toward fundraising and Human Practices. As a new approach to obtain money, we started to sell brownies on campus.

We participated in a public hearing on the impacts of 2,4-D use. As a concrete outcome of this hearing, a specialized working group was later created to advise the government on the issue.

May 2025

We took part in UFRGS Open Doors, presenting our project and discussing the 2,4-D issue with the broader community.

During this period, we also finalized the educational planning of our online course “Synthetic Biology for Beginners”, preparing scripts and didactic materials under the supervision of our Principal Investigator. These materials were later submitted to the Lumina platform for technical review and publication.

July–August 2025 – Growth and feedback

At the 2nd Brazilian Congress on Synthetic Biology, one team member presented our project prototype and received valuable feedback on both technical design and team organization.

September 2025 – Advances and setbacks

A new meeting with Helio Marchioro marked another brief victory: legal restrictions on 2,4-D were reinstated — only to be revoked again later that month.

As Helio summarized: “It is one more step, but 2,4-D is still in use.”

Mid–late 2025 – Building community

Throughout mid to late 2025, we focused on organizing the 1st Gaúcho Symposium on Biomolecular Engineering.

We engaged 16 researchers from six institutions: Universidade Federal do Rio Grande do Sul (UFRGS), Universidade Federal de Ciências da Saúde de Porto Alegre (UFCSPA), Universidade Federal de Pelotas (UFPEL), Universidade de Caxias do Sul (UCS), Universidade de São Paulo (USP), and Universidade Federal de São Paulo (UNIFESP).

Three biotechnology companies presented their work during the symposium: Bioaddera, Ecosynth, and Biosens.

We secured nine sponsors, all from the biotechnology sector — Agronomica Biotecnologia, Equipal, Promega, Pró-Análise, Servylab, Versus Biotecnologia, Biosumos, Alesco, and Exxtend.

The symposium also received institutional support from the Graduate Program of the Biotechnology Center and its Business Incubator.

The event was held in a hybrid format.

In November 2025, the symposium exceeded expectations, with 46 in-person participants, 34 hybrid attendees, and 22 remote participants, bringing together researchers, students, and professionals from across the region.

December 2025 – We are on

By the end of the year, the online course “Synthetic Biology for Beginners” was finalized by the Lumina platform team and officially published, becoming freely available online.

In the same period, we had fundraised nearly all the resources needed to attend the competition. Our vector was synthesized, and experiments were scheduled for 2026.

iGEM, here we go.
